The Swarm

The Swarm is a French horror film directed by Just Philippot and currently showing on Netflix.

It tells the story of a mother struggling to make a success of a locust farm following the suicide of her husband. Desperate to make ends meet, she discovers that feeding blood to the locusts makes them produce more quickly, yielding a more robust crop. But as the locusts get a taste for blood, things start to spin out of control.

The Swarm isn’t a great film.

I should put that out there before continuing with this review. Do not go into this film expecting a cinematic masterpiece. It is not that. But in the genre of animal horror, specifically insect horror, a film doesn’t really need to be great to be good.

The bar is kind of low.

The saving grace for this film is the commitment of its leads. Suliane Brahim does an amazing job portraying a mother on the edge trying to make enough money to support her family, whilst grappling with the fallout of her husband’s suicide. Sofian Khammes is a great support, playing the pining lover in the shadows, too blinded by his feelings to make the right call once he discovers what is going on in the locust farm.

Locusts are incredibly creepy insects. It doesn’t take a lot to make them frightening, but The Swarm does manage to make those little critters even more off-putting. There is a particular scene towards the end of the film where the daughter discovers what her mother is doing that was so creepy and disturbing it actually made me cringe.

But scenes like that are far and few between in The Swarm. For a film about blood-thirsty locusts, it is a surprisingly-gore-free film. I expected to see mutilated corpses and half-eaten people abound, but the camera more often than not pans away when a scene is becoming bloody. This decision might have been made to make the few gory scenes more shocking, but it ultimately makes The Swarm feel more like a film about grief and locust farming than a horror.

The film doesn’t handle grief well enough to place it in the same category as The Babadook and Midsommar, and so it seems to struggle to define itself.

Is it a horror? Is it a tragedy? Is it a film about the ins and outs of locust farming? The viewer doesn’t know and neither does The Swarm.

  • Rating: Five out of ten locusts
  • Would not watch again
